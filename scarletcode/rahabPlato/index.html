Rahab in the Book of Joshua receives a Makeover, not only as woman, but as metaphor for the Promised Land of the Israelites. I will use "The Many Saints of Newark" and "The Suicide Squad" to show how the artist, an agent of the feminine plane, mythologizes violence for the construction of a metaphor, in order to show how the feminine writer achieved the same in the Book of Joshua.

Audre Lorde speaks and writes about how "erotic knowledge empowers, ... becomes a lens through which we scrutinize all aspects of our existence, ...".1

She goes on to say that "pornography and eroticism ..." are "two diametrically opposed uses of the sexual." Rahab is referred to in Hebrew as "ish-zonah," or prostitute, in the text. The Septuagint, which is an Ancient Greek translation of the Old Testament, uses the word "porne," from which we get our word for "pornography," or a "depection of a harlot (prostitute.)" When the Second Chapter of the Book of Joshua begins, the very first verse explains that the spies sent out by Joshua " ... came into an harlot's house, named Rahab, and lodged there." 

		     And Joshua the son of Nun sent out of

			 Go view the land, even Jericho. And they

			 -Joshua 2:1 - King James Version

The word for the verb "spy" is "ragal," which can also mean "to tread upon garments which are to be washed and cleansed from dirt."

The problem with reading this text in any translation from the Hebrew is that much of the intended wordplay is lost in translation, as is true with any translation. The pun is that the "agents of Divine will" are charged with purging the land, but it is Rahab who purges them. The spies are reflected in the men who chase after them:

The word for "roof" is "gag," and a roof was flat in Near Eastern cultures and thus is the same concept used for the top or surface of a spiritual altar. Stalks of flax were used for linen, and threads. Metaphorically, Rahab is brining the masculine onto a spiritual plane wherein she can weave a narrative that can make sense to a masculine mind.

The Jordan River here represents the power of humanity as experienced in the feminine via sexuality. The fords are shallow places where a river or stream may be crossed. The masculine is reflected in the men pursuing the would-be spies.

This ford is the delicate balance between the Chaotic and the Ordered, a Dionysian-Apollonian equation, whereby Nietzsche claims the greatest insight is gleaned and which is the definition of great art. In a similar vein, Harold McBrayer is a vassal or puppet to Dickie.

Harold is African-American and thus can never hope to be made an equal in the Italian Mob. Yet, Chase blends Harold's position in society with that of Giuseppina. Giuseppina is the Italian feminine form of the Biblical name Joseph, which stems from the primitive root ysp, which means, "YHWH will add," or "God will grow."

Giuseppina is Dickie's Italian youthful and beautiful step-mother, whom his elderly father married in Italy. Dickie's father is an associate of the Italian Mob and is named "Hollywood Dickie." Giuseppina arouses the Erotic in the younger Dickie, whereby Dickie murders his father and takes Giuseppina as his comare, which is the Mob slang term for "mistress."

As Dickie's comare, Giuseppina grows more American, mastering English and becoming an entreprenuer; yet she is still under Dickie's thumb, mirroring the way Harold is Dickie's lackey.

Sound and waves of water are used as symbols and images for the feminine divine, or, to use Lorde's concept, the Erotic as Power. Sound is used via music, specifically, when Harold hears the revolutionary poets of the early 1970's, Chase uses the music of Gil Scott-Heron. Cigarettes are used as a symbol of addiction, but also as a symbol of spirituality.

Harold has sex with Giuseppina after she and Dickie fight; during this scene, Giuseppina and Harold share a cigarette while talking about finding their empowerment through their capacity to make money via a personal business.

Giuseppina, however, returns to Dickie, while Harold wages a war against him. Ultimately, Giuseppina is killed by Dickie; here the symbolism of waves of water is used, in that Giuseppina is drowned on the beach amid the crashing of waves from the ocean. This scene blends into a scene of a young Tony Soprano listening to music waves coming from speakers, which were given to him by Dickie. Much later in the David Chase Mob Universe, it's Tony Soprano who kills Dickie's only son, Christopher Moltisanti.
